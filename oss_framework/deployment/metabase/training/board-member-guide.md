# Metabase Analytics - Board Member Guide

**Version**: 1.0  
**Last Updated**: January 2026  
**Audience**: School Board Members, Trustees  
**Access Level**: District-Wide, Aggregated Data

---

## Overview

As a school board member, you have access to **high-level, aggregated data** that helps you:
- ✅ Monitor district-wide performance trends
- ✅ Identify equity gaps and ensure all students are served
- ✅ Make data-informed policy decisions
- ✅ Hold administration accountable for progress on strategic goals

**Your Dashboard Access**:
- **Dashboard 1**: Chronic Absenteeism Risk (district-wide, no student names)
- **Dashboard 3**: Equity Outcomes Analysis (demographic comparisons)
- **Dashboard 5**: Performance Correlations (statistical insights)

**Data Scope**: You see **aggregated data** only. **No individual student names or IDs** are visible to protect student privacy (FERPA compliance).

**Important**: Your role is **governance and policy**, not operational management. Use dashboards to:
- Set strategic goals (e.g., "Reduce chronic absenteeism by 20% over 3 years")
- Monitor progress toward goals
- Ask accountability questions of superintendent
- Inform budget and resource allocation decisions

## Section 1: Your Dashboards

### Dashboard 1: Chronic Absenteeism Risk (District-Wide View)

**Purpose**: Monitor district-wide attendance patterns and chronic absenteeism rates

**What You'll See**:

1. **Risk Distribution Chart** (Pie Chart)
   - Shows breakdown of all district students by risk level: Low / Medium / High / Critical
   - **Board Question**: "What percentage of students are at High or Critical risk? Is this improving?"

2. **Chronic Absenteeism Rate by School** (Bar Chart)
   - Compares chronic absenteeism rate across all district schools
   - **Board Question**: "Which schools have highest rates? What support do they need?"

3. **Trend Over Time** (Line Chart)
   - Shows district-wide chronic absenteeism rate over past 3 years
   - **Board Question**: "Are we on track to meet our strategic goal?"

4. **Key Metrics** (4 Cards)
   - **Total Students Monitored**: District enrollment
   - **Overall Chronic Absenteeism Rate**: % of students missing 10%+ of school days
   - **High/Critical Risk Count**: Number of students needing immediate intervention
   - **Year-over-Year Change**: Is rate improving or worsening?

**Why This Matters**:
- Chronic absenteeism is a **leading indicator** of dropout risk
- Legally required reporting metric (Every Student Succeeds Act)
- Directly impacts district accountability rating
- Research shows: Improving attendance improves graduation rates, test scores, and college enrollment

**Board Actions**:
- **Policy**: Adopt attendance improvement goals in strategic plan
- **Budget**: Allocate funds for transportation, truancy intervention, family engagement
- **Accountability**: Ask superintendent quarterly: "What interventions are working?"

---

### Dashboard 3: Equity Outcomes Analysis

**Purpose**: Identify and address opportunity and achievement gaps across demographic groups

**What You'll See**:

1. **Attendance Rate by Demographic Subgroup** (Bar Chart)
   - Compares attendance rates across: Race/Ethnicity, ELL status, SPED status, FRL (free/reduced lunch) status
   - **Board Question**: "Do all subgroups have equitable attendance outcomes?"

2. **GPA Distribution by Subgroup** (Box Plot)
   - Shows academic performance (GPA range) for each demographic group
   - **Board Question**: "Are there achievement gaps we need to address?"

3. **Discipline Disparities** (Stacked Bar)
   - Count and type of discipline incidents by race/ethnicity
   - **Board Question**: "Are discipline practices equitable? Is one group disproportionately suspended?"

4. **Opportunity Gap Table** (Table)
   - Columns: Subgroup, cohort size, % good attendance, avg GPA, % passed core classes, equity flag
   - **Board Question**: "Which groups are underperforming? What's our action plan?"

5. **Key Equity Metrics** (4 Cards)
   - **Largest Attendance Gap**: e.g., "15 percentage points between African American and Asian students"
   - **Largest GPA Gap**: e.g., "0.8 GPA points between ELL and non-ELL"
   - **Disproportionate Discipline Group**: e.g., "Hispanic students 2x more likely to be suspended"
   - **% Students in Underperforming Subgroups**: How many students are affected by gaps

**Why This Matters**:
- Federal law (Title VI, ESSA) requires districts to address equity gaps
- Opportunity gaps perpetuate systemic inequities
- Closing gaps improves **overall** district performance (rising tide lifts all boats)
- Community accountability - taxpayers want to know all students are served

**Board Actions**:
- **Policy**: Adopt equity goals (e.g., "Eliminate 50% of achievement gap in 3 years")
- **Budget**: Fund culturally responsive teaching PD, bilingual staff, restorative justice programs
- **Accountability**: Require annual equity report from superintendent
- **Community Engagement**: Hold public forums on equity data

---

### Dashboard 5: Performance Correlations

**Purpose**: Understand statistical relationships between factors affecting student success

**What You'll See**:

1. **Correlation Matrix** (Heatmap)
   - Shows strength of relationships between: Attendance, GPA, Discipline, Test Scores, Socioeconomic Status
   - **Example**: Attendance and GPA have correlation of +0.68 (strong positive)
   - **Board Insight**: "Improving attendance will likely improve GPA"

2. **Attendance ↔ GPA Correlation** (Scatter Plot)
   - Each dot = one school
   - X-axis: Average attendance rate, Y-axis: Average GPA
   - **Board Question**: "Are there schools with good attendance but low GPA? Why?"

3. **Discipline ↔ GPA Correlation** (Scatter Plot)
   - X-axis: Discipline incidents per 100 students, Y-axis: Average GPA
   - **Board Insight**: Negative correlation = more discipline incidents linked to lower GPA

4. **Key Correlations** (4 Cards)
   - **Attendance → GPA**: Correlation value and interpretation
   - **Discipline → GPA**: Correlation value and interpretation
   - **Socioeconomic Status → Outcomes**: Correlation value and interpretation
   - **Strongest Predictor**: Which factor most strongly predicts success?

**Why This Matters**:
- Helps board prioritize investments: "If we improve X, we'll likely improve Y"
- Evidence-based decision making (not anecdotal)
- Identifies leverage points for improvement

**Example Decisions**:
- If attendance strongly correlates with GPA → Invest in attendance interventions first
- If discipline weakly correlates with GPA → Don't assume stricter discipline = better academics
- If socioeconomic status strongly correlates → Invest in poverty mitigation (food, mental health, transportation)

## Section 2: Common Board Tasks

### Task 1: Prepare for Board Meeting

**Goal**: Review data before board meeting to ask informed questions

**Steps**:

1. **One week before meeting**: Superintendent sends board packet with data
2. **Open Dashboard 1**: Review chronic absenteeism metrics
   - Note: District chronic absenteeism rate, trend, schools with highest rates
3. **Open Dashboard 3**: Review equity gaps
   - Note: Largest gaps, affected subgroups
4. **Open Dashboard 5**: Review correlations
   - Note: Strongest predictors of success
5. **Prepare questions** (see examples below)

**Sample Board Questions**:
| Topic | Question |
|-------|----------|
| **Attendance** | "Chronic absenteeism increased 2% this year. What interventions are being implemented at schools with highest rates?" |
| **Equity** | "Hispanic students have 12 percentage point lower attendance than district average. What's the root cause analysis? What's the action plan?" |
| **Correlations** | "Data shows attendance is the strongest predictor of GPA. How are we allocating resources to improve attendance district-wide?" |
| **Accountability** | "Last year we set a goal to reduce chronic absenteeism by 5%. We're only down 2%. What adjustments are needed?" |

**Tip**: Ask **"what, why, how"** questions, not yes/no questions.
- ✅ "What interventions have been most effective?" (requires explanation)
- ❌ "Is attendance improving?" (yes/no, not actionable)

---

### Task 2: Set Strategic Goals (Annual Planning)

**Goal**: Use data to inform 3-5 year strategic plan goals

**Steps**:

1. **Review 3-year trends**:
   - Dashboard 1: Chronic absenteeism trend line
   - Dashboard 3: Equity gap trend (are gaps closing?)
2. **Identify baseline**:
   - Example: "Current chronic absenteeism rate is 18%"
3. **Set SMART goal**:
   - **Specific**: Reduce chronic absenteeism rate
   - **Measurable**: From 18% to 13%
   - **Achievable**: 5 percentage point drop over 3 years (research-based)
   - **Relevant**: Aligns with state accountability, improves graduation rate
   - **Time-bound**: By June 2029
4. **Adopt in board resolution**:
   > "RESOLVED: The Board adopts a strategic goal to reduce chronic absenteeism from 18% to 13% by June 2029. The Superintendent shall present annual progress reports each September."

**Example Strategic Goals**:
- **Attendance**: "Reduce chronic absenteeism from 18% to 13% by 2029"
- **Equity**: "Eliminate 50% of attendance gap between Hispanic and White students by 2027"
- **Achievement**: "Increase district-wide average GPA from 2.5 to 2.8 by 2028"

**Frequency**: Annual (typically at summer board retreat)

---

### Task 3: Budget Allocation Decisions

**Goal**: Use data to inform budget priorities

**Steps**:

1. **Review Dashboard 3**: Identify equity gaps
   - Example: "ELL students have 15 percentage point lower attendance"
2. **Review Dashboard 5**: Identify strongest correlations
   - Example: "Attendance correlates +0.68 with GPA (strongest predictor)"
3. **Identify intervention programs** that address gaps:
   - Bilingual family liaisons (improve ELL attendance)
   - Transportation assistance (improve low-income attendance)
   - Restorative justice (reduce suspensions, keep students in school)
4. **In budget discussion**, advocate for programs with data support:
   > "Dashboard shows ELL students have significantly lower attendance. I propose we fund 3 additional bilingual family liaisons ($180K) to address this gap."

**Data-Informed Budget Advocacy**:
| Gap Identified | Proposed Solution | Cost | Expected Outcome |
|----------------|-------------------|------|------------------|
| ELL attendance gap | 3 bilingual family liaisons | $180K | Close 5 percentage point gap |
| High suspension rate | Restorative justice coordinator | $95K | Reduce suspensions 30%, improve attendance |
| Transportation barrier | Late bus service | $50K | Reduce chronic absenteeism by 2% |

**Frequency**: Annual budget cycle (typically January-June)

---

### Task 4: Community Accountability

**Goal**: Share data transparently with community to build trust

**Steps**:

1. **Prepare community presentation** (15-20 minutes):
   - Slide 1: Title - "District Data Update: Student Attendance and Equity"
   - Slide 2: Dashboard 1 screenshot - "18% of students chronically absent, down from 20% last year"
   - Slide 3: Dashboard 3 screenshot - "Achievement gaps persist for ELL and low-income students"
   - Slide 4: Actions taken - "Hired family liaisons, implemented early warning system, increased mental health support"
   - Slide 5: Next steps - "Continue interventions, expand successful programs, monitor progress"
2. **Present at**:
   - Board meeting (public session)
   - Parent-Teacher Association (PTA) meeting
   - Community forum
3. **Invite questions** and feedback

**Communication Tips**:
- Be **transparent** about challenges (don't hide bad news)
- Show **progress** and **action** (not just problems)
- Use **plain language** (avoid jargon like "correlations" - say "linked to")
- Celebrate **wins** ("9th grade attendance improved 8%!")

**Frequency**: Quarterly update to community

## Section 3: Interpreting the Data

### Understanding Chronic Absenteeism

**Definition**: Student misses 10% or more of school days (18+ days per 180-day year)

**Why 10% threshold?**
- Attendance Below 90% Is a Problem. A student with 90% attendance has missed 18 days of school, the equivalent of almost 4 full school weeks. The following data points make it clear that 90% attendance poses a significant problem for students.

**National Context**:
- **Before COVID**: ~15% of U.S. students chronically absent
- **Post-COVID**: ~25-30% of students chronically absent (national crisis)
- **Your district**: [Check Dashboard 1 for current rate]

**Impact**:
- Students with 90% attendance are **2-3x more likely to drop out**
- Chronic absenteeism in 6th grade predicts dropout better than test scores
- Every 10 days absent = ~0.5 GPA points lower

**Board Role**: Set ambitious but achievable goals, fund interventions, monitor progress.

---

### Understanding Equity Gaps

**What is an equity gap?**
- Difference in outcomes between demographic subgroups
- Example: "African American students have 75% attendance vs. 88% district average" = **13 percentage point gap**

**How large is too large?**
- **Small**: <3 percentage points (may be statistical noise)
- **Moderate**: 3-7 percentage points (monitor, investigate)
- **Large**: >7 percentage points (**policy action required**)

**Root Causes to Consider**:
- **Systemic Factors**: Implicit bias in discipline, lack of culturally responsive teaching, language barriers
- **Structural Factors**: Transportation access, food insecurity, housing instability, healthcare access
- **School Climate**: Safety concerns, bullying, lack of belonging, low expectations

**Board Actions**:
- Commission **root cause analysis** (qualitative + quantitative data)
- Adopt **equity policy** (e.g., require equity impact statements for all policies)
- Fund **targeted interventions** (not just "programs for all")
- Require **annual equity audit** from superintendent

---

### Understanding Correlations

**What does correlation mean?**
- Statistical measure of relationship between two variables
- Range: -1.0 (perfect negative) to +1.0 (perfect positive)
- **0.0** = no relationship
- **+0.3 to +0.5** = weak to moderate positive relationship
- **+0.6 to +0.8** = strong positive relationship
- **+0.9 to +1.0** = very strong positive relationship

**Example Interpretations**:
| Correlation | Interpretation | Board Implication |
|-------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Attendance ↔ GPA: **+0.68** | Strong positive: higher attendance linked to higher GPA | **Invest in attendance improvement** - likely to boost GPA |
| Discipline ↔ GPA: **-0.42** | Moderate negative: more discipline incidents linked to lower GPA | Discipline may be symptom, not cause. Focus on root issues. |
| Socioeconomic Status ↔ GPA: **+0.55** | Moderate positive: higher income linked to higher GPA | Address poverty barriers (food, housing, mental health) |

**⚠️ Correlation ≠ Causation**: Just because two things are correlated doesn't mean one causes the other.
- Example: Ice cream sales correlate with drowning deaths (both happen in summer, but ice cream doesn't cause drowning)
- Use correlations to **inform**, not **dictate** decisions

## Section 4: Board Governance Best Practices

### DO:
- ✅ Focus on **policy and goals**, not day-to-day operations
- ✅ Ask **"what, why, how"** questions of superintendent
- ✅ Use data to **inform decisions**, not replace judgment
- ✅ Advocate for **equity** and **transparency**
- ✅ Set **measurable goals** and hold superintendent accountable

### DON'T:
- ❌ Micromanage schools or staff (that's the superintendent's job)
- ❌ Request **individual student data** (FERPA violation, inappropriate)
- ❌ Use data to **punish** schools/staff (focus on support and improvement)
- ❌ Make **knee-jerk decisions** based on one data point (look at trends)
- ❌ Ignore **context** (e.g., school A has different population than school B)

---

### Data + Human Judgment

**Data should inform, not dictate.**

**Example**:
- **Data shows**: School A has 25% chronic absenteeism (highest in district)
- **Bad response**: "Fire the principal and cut their budget"
- **Good response**: "What are the unique challenges at School A? Do they have higher % of homeless students, ELL, or foster youth? What additional support do they need?"

**Remember**: Behind every data point is a child, a family, a story. Use data to **understand and support**, not to label and punish.

## Section 5: Common Questions

### Q: Can I see data for individual students?

**No.** Board members do **not** have access to individual student data (FERPA privacy law).

You see:
- ✅ Aggregated data (district-wide, school-level)
- ✅ Subgroup data (demographics, no names)

You do NOT see:
- ❌ Student names or ID numbers
- ❌ Individual student attendance or grades
- ❌ Individual discipline records

**Why?** Board's role is **governance**, not case management. Operational decisions (e.g., individual student interventions) are superintendent's responsibility.

---

### Q: How often is data updated?

**Dashboard Refresh**: Daily at 6 AM (shows yesterday's data)

**Source Data**: 
- Attendance: Exported from SIS (Aeries) nightly
- Grades: Updated weekly during term, final at end of term
- Discipline: Entered real-time by administrators

**Lag**: You see data from yesterday. For real-time data, ask superintendent to pull custom report.

---

### Q: What if data doesn't match what I've heard from parents or community?

**Possible Reasons**:
1. **Anecdotal vs. systematic**: Individual stories may not reflect overall trends
2. **Perception vs. reality**: Community perception may lag behind actual improvements
3. **Data error**: Possible (but rare) - verify with superintendent

**Action**:
1. Ask superintendent to verify data accuracy
2. Request deeper analysis (e.g., "Why does community perceive attendance as worsening when data shows improvement?")
3. Consider: Is there a **communication gap**? Does community need more transparency?

**Best Practice**: Use data to **confirm or challenge** anecdotes, not dismiss community concerns.

---

### Q: How do we compare to other districts?

**Benchmarking**:
- Dashboard shows **district average** (your internal baseline)
- For external comparison, request superintendent to provide:
  - **State average** (how you compare statewide)
  - **Similar districts** (demographics, size, location)
  - **National average** (for context)

**Tip**: Comparing to similar districts is more meaningful than comparing to state average (apples to apples).

### Issue: Dashboard looks different than last time

**Possible Causes**:
- Dashboard was updated (features added)

**Action**: If confused, contact analytics team: analytics@district.edu for walkthrough.

---

### Issue: Data doesn't make sense

**Example**: "Dashboard shows chronic absenteeism improved, but superintendent reported it worsened"

**Action**:
1. Check **date range filter** (are you comparing same time periods?)
2. Check **school filter** (are you looking at district-wide or one school?)
3. Ask superintendent to reconcile discrepancy at next board meeting
